The story of a blind dog

How it happened?

It happened overnight one day she is fine playing, jumping and running, second day she is visibly sadder, as the sadness progressed we then realized she has a white spot on her right eye, we then took her to the vet, which prescribed us some medication. Sisi took that medication for 1 week she was feeling better, however suddenly the second eyes had the spot, and her pupils started to get bigger. Calling the doctor suggested to take her to a special vet which after the consultation said: The right eye has 70% pressure and it cant be saved, however the left eye they could try to save it, but the chances are small, and she would have to live on medication for 1-2 years till her eye would give up and then it will need to be removed.

There was no good option they all lead to the same result which meant blindness.

Why it happened?

Usually it happens from an accident, such as falling or similar things in dogs case, but as the Vet said Sisis was genetic, is possible that her brothers maybe suffering from this too. But is a good thing, that she has been spayed otherwise her babies would have carried this gene, which unfortunately is common in terrier breeds.

It can happen overnight just like it happened for Sisi, and in some cases can take 2 months or more to develop into blindness and some can even be 2 weeks.

What is Glaucoma?

Glaucoma is a condition in which pressure is placed on the eye, causing fluid drainage in the eye. If the condition becomes chronic or persists without treatment, it will eventually cause permanent damage to the optic nerve, resulting in blindness. Symptoms,are as follows:

High pressure within the eye

Dilated pupil or pupil does not respond to light

Cloudy appearance at front of the eye

Vision loss

What is Lens luxation?

Many cases of lens luxation are hereditary and caused by a weakness in the threads holding the lens in place. This condition is called primary lens luxation and is common in the Jack Russell and other terriers. It is also occasionally seen in other breeds and crossbreeds, unfortunately primary lens luxation affects both eyes, although not necessarily at the same time.

In dogs suffering from lens luxation (dislocation), such as in Sisis case, the lens shifts out of position and moves either into the front or into the back of the eye. If the lens becomes trapped in the front of the eye, it can cause water-logging of the window of the eye and an increase in pressure within the eye (glaucoma).

Eyes that have lost vision irreversibly due to lens luxation are not suitable for lens removal. Because many eyes with lens luxation are painful, it may be necessary to consider removal of the eye under these circumstances.
